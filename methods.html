Deal Grader

The one-line method statement baked into every Deal Grader board payload (app.py, served at /api/deal-board.json):

Quoted from the deal-board payload · app.py

“Each eligible skater contract is scored as-of its signing date: fair value = 0.75×term-matched WAR value + 0.25×production comps, with the WAR anchor frozen from the signing-summer outcomes ledger and the comp pool restricted to earlier signings (no hindsight). Letters are cap-share bands centered on realized-outcome quantiles from 1,721 historical skater signings (held-out Spearman +0.564). Older deals with no frozen WAR anchor fall back to a current-model reconstruction — not a frozen signing-date forecast — and their receipts say so. Goalies are not yet graded.”

The model, in its own words

The grader's module docstring is the methodology of record — the v1.3 re-anchor: the fair-value blend, letter bands fit on realized-outcome quantiles, and the acceptance gates. Quoted verbatim:

Quoted from the nhl_deal_grader.py module docstring

Deal Grader -- the letter grade on a contract, framed as a bet.

Reuses the CALIBRATED market value (nhl_market_value.fair_contract: an aging-curve WAR walk x $/WAR by term) as the value anchor, so it doesn't tank scorers and young players the way a naive WAR-surplus would. The grade compares the actual AAV to that fair AAV, then layers the validated explainers: finishing (nhl_finishing), age/term, and after-tax (nhl_jock_tax).

v1 honesty: the value anchor is WAR-based, which compresses pure scorers (RAPM under-credits goal-first wingers). When the player grades as a real finisher we flag the model-vs-market gap and soften the overpay rather than calling a sniper's deal a value loss -- the position-specific calibration the spec calls for. Goalies are out of scope here (own GSAx spine).

v1.3 re-anchor (outcome-calibrated; tools/deal_grade_calibration.py --candidate is the acceptance gate, fit on <=2021 signings, validated on 2022-23 held-out):

  • fair = BLEND of the two anchors (w=0.25 comps, 0.75 WAR) instead of max(anchors) -- the max() was an upward ratchet (fair overstated realized value by +$0.70M/yr; the blend is within $0.10M on the fit cohort and $0.17M held-out).
  • letter bands re-centered on REALIZED-OUTCOME quantiles (isotonic level-matching on the fit cohort), so a letter's level means what it says: an "A" deal's expected outcome is a top ~6% realized surplus (~+$2M/yr in today's cap), and a league-median deal grades "B" (the median NHL deal realizes ~-$1.8M/yr vs production value in its first 3 seasons -- teams pay for the past; the letters now price that honestly).
  • gates: held-out letter-bin monotonicity PASS, held-out Spearman +0.569 (old grader +0.413, its WAR anchor alone +0.597 -- but that comparison is partly circular since realized surplus is WAR-denominated), pooled "A" median realized +$1.58M/yr.

v1.4 as-of scoring (the no-hindsight upgrade; same acceptance gate re-run, held-out Spearman +0.573 vs the pre-registered +0.569 -- within noise -- and old grader +0.409):

  • the WAR anchor is FROZEN at the signing date where an outcomes-ledger row exists (_as_of_walk_war3): the trailing-3 WAR the model would have seen that summer, walked through the aging curve in the signing-year cap frame. Fresh signings fall through to the current cache, which IS as-of for a deal signed today; only older deals with no outcomes row use the current-model reconstruction fallback, and their receipt says so.
  • the comp pool excludes signings after the deal's signing year (no-hindsight comps).
  • RFA/UFA status at signing is resolved from the EXPIRING deal's expiry label (status_at_signing), not guessed from age.
  • letter bands are CAP-SHARES (edges fit at the $104M cap, applied at the scoring cap), so a 2016 grade and a 2026 grade mean the same slice of a team's cap.
  • one-year deals now grade (with a bands-were-fit-on-multi-year caveat note).
  • every grade is snapshotted append-only at first sight (nhl_deal_grade_snapshot, schema-enforced immutable) with a model-version hash, so the public record of what the model said accrues from v1.4 forward.

WAR → dollars ($ per WAR)

Production value converts WAR to cap dollars through the league-minimum floor (nhl_market_value.py; current baked constants shown — refit with fit_dollars_per_war, and a test asserts the baked constant stays within tolerance of a live re-fit):

production_value$ = $775,000 + $4,710,846 × max(WAR, 0)
surplus$          = production_value$ − cap_hit$
  • Skater $/WAR: $4,710,846 ($4.71M) per WAR (nhl_market_value.DOLLARS_PER_WAR).
  • Goalie $/WAR: $1,990,000 ($1.99M) per WAR (nhl_market_value.GOALIE_DOLLARS_PER_WAR) — goalie WAR (GSAx-based) runs on its own scale, so goalies get their own rate.
  • Replacement floor: $775,000 (nhl_market_value.LEAGUE_MIN).

Honest scope

Quoted from the nhl_market_value.py module docstring

  • Timing mismatch. WAR is historical (the seasons we imported, 2022-25); the cap hit is the player's CURRENT contract. A star who just signed a big extension off a limited or down sample (Rantanen, Cooley, Faber, Kaprizov) will look "overpaid" because the deal bets on a future our WAR can't see. Surplus is most honest for players whose contract overlaps the measured window. We expose seasons so the caller can weight accordingly.
    • Position blind spots. RAPM-based WAR can compress elite defensemen (Makar/Faber read low) and pure finishers / aging snipers (Ovechkin reads negative); those players' production value is understated here.
    • Single-season WAR is noisy -- prefer >= 2 seasons and present ranges, not false precision. The leaderboards default to a 2-season minimum.

Extension-stack cap hits — FIXED on the card (2026-07-08; found by the anomaly sweep 2026-07-07)

  • The bug: an extension signed while the current deal has a season left makes the CapWages cache carry the FUTURE deal's AAV as today's cap_hit (years_remaining > 8 is the tell — impossible for one contract). Live cases, all signed Jul 1 2026 with 2027-28 starts: Demidov ($9.15M shown vs his real ~$0.94M ELC — the market's biggest bargain graded as market-rate), Luostarinen ($5M vs $3M), Foerster ($7.1M vs $3.75M).

Verified: Demidov reads +$4.36M/yr surplus on $0.94M. Tests pin the Demidov case and that unstacked players are untouched.

Fix belongs at a corrected READ layer (view) so every consumer agrees; the raw cache stays untouched as the record of what CapWages said.

Extensions really do overpay — the gated study PASSES (2026-07-07)

  • Upgrade of the exploratory finding below (same date): "extensions overpay more than market deals" is now a VALIDATED claim. Registration frozen in tools/extension_overpay_study.py BEFORE any realized-frame number was computed: context buckets frozen from the exploratory cut (extension = signed ≥120d before the deal's July 1; market-window = −60..+45d; in-season/late reported, never gated), realized-outcome frame (mean total_war over the deal's first min(3, term) seasons, missing seasons = 0, valued at $775k + $4.71M/WAR; cost = AAV in START-season today-cap frame — the grader's signing-year frame mechanically inflates early extensions in a rising cap, i.e. biases toward the hypothesis, so it was demoted to a sensitivity), quality control = 2-season pre-signing WAR terciles × age bands (≤25 / 26–29 / 30+), seeded 10k bootstrap.
  • Population: 1,627 historical skater deals ≥$1M×2yr starting 2011–2024 (365 extensions, 974 market-window, 288 in-season/late; excluded: 202 goalie/no-bio, 19 no pre-signing WAR, 4 unparseable dates).
  • G1 PASS — raw realized gap: −$0.84M/yr, CI [−1.17, −0.52]; extensions realize −$2.25M/yr vs market-window −$1.41M/yr (gate: ≤ −$0.25M and CI excluding 0).
  • G2 PASS — net of quality × age (9/9 cells usable, n=1,339): −$0.84M/yr [−1.16, −0.54]. The stratified gap EQUALS the raw gap — star/age selection explains none of it.
  • G3 PASS — both eras: −$0.86M (2011–2017), −$0.75M (2018–2024).
  • Sensitivities: signing-year frame −$0.97M/yr (the registered frame was the conservative choice). Overpay-RATE frame narrows (80.3% vs 76.6% realize below the line — outcomes are harsh on everyone; the calibration already said the median deal realizes ~−$1.8M/yr); the dollar gap is primary, as registered.
  • Mechanism = TERM, and it matters for the copy: within the same term bucket the gap nearly vanishes (2–3yr −$0.30M; 4–6yr −$0.06M) and REVERSES on 7+ deals (+$0.43M — extension megadeals age BETTER than July-1 megadeals, n=106 vs 73). Extensions overpay by CHOOSING more years — term is where surplus dies, and early extensions buy more of it. Term was registered as a mediator (not a confound), so it does not gate — but honest phrasing is "mostly because they buy more years," never "extending early is dumb." Age pattern: worst at ≤25 (−$1.14M, the big young-core bets) and 30+ (−$0.99M, loyalty extensions), mildest 26–29 (−$0.24M). In-season/late deals realize best of all (−$0.73M/yr) — weak-leverage, prove-it pricing.
  • Bucket semantics note: "market-window" means signed at market TIMING (mid-May–Aug), own-team expiry re-signs included — the comparison is early-vs-at-market (the user's team-control question), not changed-teams-vs-stayed.
  • Shipped: one sentence on the deal-grader hero tile ("Even early extensions pay it: they age worse than July-1 deals — the extra years are the price."). Reopening condition: re-run the registered script as new seasons accrue; the claim is descriptive of 2011–2024 signings.

"Is there even enough +WAR to sign only fair deals?" — NO: the supply proof, plus the extension surprise (2026-07-07)

  • The user's two follow-ups to the entry below: (1) does the league even HAVE enough positive-WAR players on signable contracts for 32 teams to reach the cap paying only fair prices? (2) aren't many graded deals signed under team control (extensions, RFA years) rather than in a 32-team July-1 auction — and if so, is "winner's curse of an auction" the right mechanism?
  • (1) The supply arithmetic — overpaying the line is MANDATORY, not a choice (run 2026-07-07 on the live DB; 648 rostered players with WAR + contract): the graded pool (AAV ≥ $1M, 2yr+; n=505) holds 308 of the league's 359 +WAR. Buying ALL of it exactly at the line ($775k floor + $4.71M/WAR) plus minimum-salary roster fills funds ~$2.02B of payroll. Lineup impact (nhl_lineup_impact.py, 2026-06-15) — how a swap moves the team

  • The shared primitive behind the live impact preview (add/remove/swap a body in the trade, lineup, or FA sim and watch the team get faster / bigger / more defensive) and the realistic trade-target finder. A team's dimension is the TOI-WEIGHTED AVERAGE of its rostered players' league percentiles (nhl_team_needs.player_features) on that dimension, so a swap recomputes cleanly and re-ranks against the other 31 teams. Dimensions: skating, size, physicality, scoring, defense.
  • HONEST SCOPE (the line that keeps it trustworthy): these are ROSTER-COMPOSITION dimensions — what the players on the sheet are, on average, vs the league. NOT a re-run of RAPM, NOT a standings projection. "Does this make us faster on paper", not "worth +N points". Team-RESULTS dims (on-ice xGF/xGA, PP, PK) can't be honestly re-derived from one swap, so they are NOT faked. A player too thin to grade contributes nothing to a dimension (coverage shrinks), not a zero.
  • REPLACEMENT BACKFILL (added 2026-06-15, the honesty fix): a spot vacated WITHOUT a return (a salary dump, a UFA walking) is backfilled at replacement level (replacement_profile = the bottom-TOI-quartile average per dim, taking a typical regular's minutes) so the roster isn't scored as smaller-and-better. With it, letting BUF's skilled UFAs walk correctly DROPS skating #5→#11, scoring #4→#16 while physicality rises (replacements are grinders) — the honest "grittier but far less skilled" picture. A balanced 1-for-1 backfills nothing.

Trade evaluator (nhl_trade_eval.py, 2026-06-15) — is this deal SMART, not just legal

  • The decision layer CapFriendly's sim never had: evaluate_trade(conn, trade) takes an arbitrary multi-team deal (players + picks + retention) and grades it per team on three axes, then calls fairness + cap-legality. It adds NO new calibration — it WIRES already-calibrated parts: value = nhl_player_asset_value projected surplus + the pick_value_curve; cap = team_cap_space + retention math; fit = the trade_fit need-vs-strength score; suggested add = the dollar margin mapped back onto the pick curve.
  • THE MATH, made honest: value is CONSERVED (a player's surplus + the cap the retention hands the receiver transfers whole, so the per-team deltas sum to ~0), which is why "fairness" is the SPREAD of the deltas, not an absolute. Cap relief to the sender is cap_hit - retained_annual (they keep the retained slice as dead money); only a contract running into 2026-27 carries a planning-season hit.
  • WHAT IT REFUSES TO FAKE: no NMC/NTC clause data exists, so termed players are FLAGGED ("verify clause"), never silently cleared; cap is headline-level (no LTIR/bonus-overage engine, flagged); the 50-SPC limit isn't enforced; the superstar tier drops confidence to low (uncalibrated). Retention is capped at 50% and 3 slots/team (counted from nhl_dead_cap), and over-the-cap results fail legality. Confidence = high / medium (thin dump comps or no fit inputs) / low (superstar or a missing valuation).
  • WORKED EXAMPLE (live): BUF dumps Greenway (25% retained) + a 4th for a 7th — frees $3.0M, value +$1.6M, but the engine calls it lopsided ("SEA needs ~$1.6M more — a 2nd") WHILE noticing Greenway's size/PK fills Seattle's needs (fit +2.7). That multi-axis read — overpay on value, but a fit-and-cap reason to do it — is the payoff no cap site gives.
  • Dependencies are injectable (cap_space, profiles, feats, pick_curve) like trade_fit, so the engine is testable headless. Tests: test_nhl_trade_eval.py (10: conservation, retention cap math, fairness + suggested add, retention-slot and over-cap legality, clause flag, confidence).
  • SHIPPED end to end: POST /api/nhl-trade-eval (nhl_trade_eval_payload, reusing the cached team-needs inputs) and a "Trade evaluator" tab in front-office.html — pick two teams, tap players from their rosters into outgoing lanes with a 0-50% retention slider, add picks, Evaluate. Market value (nhl_player_market_value, nhl_market_value.py) — WAR meets the cap hit

  • What it is. The value half (WAR) and the cost half (cap hit, imported from CapWages with nhlId verification) finally meet. A player's stable, multi-year- average total WAR is converted to a production value in dollars, and the cap hit subtracted to give surplus — the team-friendliness of the contract: production_value = $0.775M league-min + $5.10M/WAR × max(WAR, 0); surplus = production_value − cap_hit (positive = bargain). (The $5.10M here is historical; current: skater $4.71M re-fit 2026-06-27 / goalie $1.99M re-fit 2026-07-01 — constants are now generated from code.)
  • Why this shape, not a raw regression. A least-squares line of cap hit on WAR has a shallow slope (~$2.6M/WAR) because entry-level and minimum deals pin the bottom — that slope makes every star look overpaid (no contract could justify $12M), which is a scale artifact, not a finding. Instead we anchor a replacement player at the league minimum and fit one marginal rate through that fixed floor: rate = Σ(war·(cap−floor)) / Σ(war²) ≈ $5.10M/WAR. That sits between the shallow marginal slope ($2.6M) and the elite-implied rate (McDavid $12.5M / 3.3 WAR ≈ $3.8M; top UFA stars cluster $4–5M per WAR of cap hit) and is close to the public ~$4M-per-win open-market figure.
  • Face validity (the calibration check). The elite tier prices sensibly: McDavid +$3.2M surplus (publicly considered a steal — the model agrees), Matthews −$0.6M / MacKinnon −$0.4M (≈ fair). Top bargains are real value deals (Giroux, Hyman, Foegele, ELC kids like Benson/Hayton); worst are aging stars and just-signed extensions.
  • Honest scope — the two caveats that matter.
    1. Timing mismatch. WAR is historical (the imported seasons, 2022-26); the cap hit is the player's current contract. A star who just signed a big extension off a limited or down sample (Rantanen, Cooley, Faber, Kaprizov) reads as an "overpay" because the deal bets on a future the WAR can't see. Surplus is most honest where the contract overlaps the measured window; the view exposes seasons and the app flags large negatives.
    2. Position blind spots. RAPM-based WAR compresses elite defensemen (Makar/Faber read low) and pure finishers / aging snipers (Ovechkin reads negative); their production value is understated here.
    3. Single-season WAR is noisy → leaderboards default to ≥ 2 seasons and surplus is meant as a range, not a point estimate.
  • Goalies (nhl_goalie_market_value). Goalies get their OWN rate because GSAx-based goalie WAR is on a different, inflated scale (~2×: an elite season is +4..+5, not +2..+3). The same floor-anchored fit gives $1.91M/WAR (vs $5.10M skater) (both historical; current: skater $4.71M re-fit 2026-06-27 / goalie $1.99M re-fit 2026-07-01 — constants are now generated from code) — the scale washes out, and the elite tier prices sensibly (Shesterkin −$1.1M and Hellebuyck ≈$0 on their big deals, Sorokin ≈fair, Thompson +$2.5M value). Goalie value is restricted to the skater WAR window (recent seasons) so a current contract is judged against current ability — without it, goalie WAR's 2009-25 span lets retired goalies (Lundqvist, Rask, Price) pollute the board (a test asserts Lundqvist is absent). Honest: goalie WAR is volatile and small-sample — surplus is a wide range. Surfaced via nhl_market_value, nhl_contract_leaders (group=goalie/all), /api/nhl-market?group=goalie, and Ask ("best goalie contracts", "is Shesterkin overpaid"). Team cap summary stays skater-only for now (goalies aren't in the shift data, so team attribution needs a games join — future).
  • Self-honesty guarantee. fit_dollars_per_war / fit_goalie_dollars_per_war recompute the rates from the live DB; tests assert the baked $5.10M / $1.91M constants (historical; the tests now guard the current $4.71M / $1.99M constants, generated from code) stay within 10% of a fresh fit as WAR and contracts change. Each view's dollars key off the same rounded WAR it displays, so the cards are self-consistent.
  • Skater fit scoped to current rosters (2026-06-15). The contract import covers ALL rated players (15-season window) so GM contract history has the data, so nhl_contracts carries stale rows for retired/departed players. When CapWages began parsing those pages to (stale or junk) cap hits, ~741 non-current skaters flooded the skater $/WAR fit and dragged it from $5.46M to $4.42M — below the 10% guard, a false alarm (the then-baked $5.25M was never wrong; historical — current: skater $4.71M re-fit 2026-06-27). fit_dollars_per_war now restricts to nhl_current_roster players (_current_player_filter), the true current market; the goalie fit is deliberately NOT scoped (its current sample is too small to fit stably and it wasn't polluted). The fit is validation/re-bake only — production reads the baked constant — so this changed no shipped value.

Aging curve + WAR projection (nhl_aging.py) — valuing a contract's future

  • The gap it closes. Market value compares a current contract to historical WAR — so a young breakout reads undervalued and a long veteran extension reads fine when its out-years are decline. Projection ages WAR forward across the remaining contract years and re-values the deal.
  • Method (delta). Pair each player's consecutive seasons, take ΔWAR, bucket by the player's age, average, then smooth with a count-weighted triangular moving average (±2 ages). The curve rises through the early 20s, peaks ~24-25, plateaus to ~30, and declines with acceleration after 32 (−0.35 WAR/yr by age 38) — the canonical NHL shape. project_war walks a stable WAR forward by these deltas; project_contract values each projected year and compares the per-year average to the (flat) cap hit. Face validity: McDavid (28) still projects an elite bargain; a 22-year-old ages up (the undervalued-young fix); a 34-year-old's out-years crater (you are paying for decline).
  • Honest scope (big). Only ~4 seasons of WAR (2022-26) → the curve is coarse and the tails (very young / late 30s) rest on few players. Survivorship bias: decliners lose ice time or leave, so the surviving sample ages "better" than reality and the delta method understates decline — projections are directional, with widening uncertainty each year. Skaters only (goalies age differently and are not projected). Surfaced on nhl_market_value, the player card (projection block), and the Ask single-player answer ("aged forward, it projects to …").
  • Tests: test_nhl_aging.py — projection math (ages up young / down old, value arithmetic, clamping), real-curve canonical shape (young > old, late-30s < 0), app integration (skater has a projection, goalie does not).

  • Cap-management roster optimizer (nhl_cap_optimizer.py). The most WAR you can buy under a budget: an exact 0/1 knapsack (pure-Python DP over the budget in $100k units) maximizing summed WAR (or surplus) with summed cap hit ≤ budget. Correctness is tested against a hand-checked optimum (A$1M/1.0 + B$2M/2.5 = 3.5 WAR beats C$3M/3.0). Forwards = skaters that aren't D/G (nhl_player_bio.position).

Front-office validation + honesty audit (front_office_validation.py) — what does and doesn't hold up

  • The point. The market-value / aging / cap layer sits on WAR. This stress-tests that layer out-of-sample and writes down the failures a skeptic would raise — the honesty bar is reporting what does not validate, not just what does.
  • Aging curve, out-of-sample — does not beat a flat baseline (the honest null). Hold out the most recent season-transition, fit the curve on the earlier ones, and predict the held-out year's WAR. Against a naive "next season = this season" forecast: flat RMSE 0.681 vs aging RMSE 0.682 (−0.07%) over 505 players. The ~0.1 WAR/yr aging signal is swamped by season-to-season variance (RMSE ≈ 0.68). So the aging curve is not a single-year forecaster — its value is the multi-year cumulative shape (a 6-yr deal accrues ~0.5 WAR of drift), and projections are labelled directional. A test asserts the model never claims a big OOS win.
  • Value persistence — the signal surplus rests on is real. Early-window average WAR vs later-window average WAR: r ≈ 0.54, measured on the re-estimated part of WAR (the fixed career-finishing constant is excluded — folding it in inflates this to ~0.70, a constant correlated with itself, so the with-finishing figure is reported separately on /api/reliability, not as the headline). Valuable players stay valuable, moderately — so surplus isn't ranking noise; but single seasons are noisy, which is why the model uses the stable multi-year average.
  • Adversarial honesty audit (7 enumerated failure modes). Stated plainly, not buried: circularity (rate fit to cap hits → the independent anchor is WAR's external validity + persistence, not market self-agreement); season-sensitivity ($/WAR moved 12% on one season → self-check + ranges); aging-single-year (the null above); survivorship (delta method understates decline → veteran out-years if anything optimistic); timing-mismatch (historical WAR vs current cap → per-player flags + aging projection); scope (skaters-only optimizer/projection, goalie-less team cap); goalie-volatility (small-sample → wide ranges).

xG RAPM (nhl_player_rapm_season) — time-weighted, offense + defense

  • What it is: a time-weighted ridge regression over every 5v5 stint (a continuous interval with a fixed ten skaters). The response is each team's xG rate (per 60), weighted by stint TOI, so the model captures shot volume (play-driving), not just per-shot quality. Two columns per player — an offense rating (effect on his team's xGF/60) and a defense rating (suppressing the opponent's xGF/60); total = offense + defense. A league-average intercept (unregularized) makes ratings deviations from average. Built pure-Python (nhl_rapm.py): matrix-free conjugate gradient over ~300k stints/season.
  • Built-in sanity: the fitted intercept comes out at 2.56 xGF/60 — the actual league 5v5 rate — so the baseline is right and player ratings are honest deviations (a test asserts |mean total| < 0.5). Ridge λ = 400, min 300 5v5 min.
  • Validation: 2024-25 totals → Jack Hughes, Werenski, Cirelli, Ekholm, MacKinnon, Adam Fox (the Norris winner); 2023-24 → McDavid, Palat, Panarin, Staal, McDonagh, MacKinnon. Crucially the split is right: pure scorers top offense (MacKinnon off +0.5), shutdown players top defense (Staal/McDonagh/ Cirelli) — exactly what a two-way model should show. (Single-season RAPM is noisy and stabilizes over multi-year samples; a few mid-minute players still rank high.)
  • Honest scope: 5v5 only; per-60 xG impact; single-season; no explicit zone-start term (stints average over zones). Coverage limited to imported-shift seasons. Win-scaled into WAR below.
  • Tests: test_nhl_rapm.py — CG matches a dense solve on the same system, recovers a synthetic offensive signal, total = offense + defense, distribution centered near 0, elite drivers in the top 20, recipe routes.

  • Single-season RAPM is noisy. Pooling every shift-season's stints (2022-26) into one ridge fit gives each player a single coefficient from his entire sample (3000+ min for regulars), far more stable than any one year. Leaders: M. Tkachuk, Bergeron (the two-way legend), B. Tkachuk, McDavid (3528 min sample), Matthews, Adam Fox. Same off/def/total per-60 units. Built with --multiyear.

5v5 xG WAR (nhl_player_war_season) — the top of the ladder

  • What it is: the time-weighted RAPM converted to Goals/Wins Above Replacement. Three steps: (1) rate → counting stat (per-60 RAPM × 5v5 TOI); (2) above-average → above-replacement (a replacement skater is ~0.15 goals/60 below average at 5v5, so that gap is added); (3) goals → wins (~6 goals/win; xG is goal-calibrated, so xG impact reads as goals 1:1). off/def goals-above-average are also exposed.
  • Validation (magnitudes + leaders): 2023-24 → MacKinnon +2.66 (the Hart winner) and Quinn Hughes (the Norris winner) both top it, with McDavid, Panarin, McDonagh, Matthews, Karlsson; 2024-25 → Werenski, MacKinnon, Morrissey, Dahlin, Hughes. Magnitudes are sane for a 5v5 component (best ~+2.5–3.0 WAR, median ~+0.3, worst regulars ~−1.5) — the right fraction of an all-situations WAR.
  • COMPLETE WAR → nhl_player_war_total_season = 5v5 + special teams + penalties. Three honest components, each a regression or a direct count — no exposure fudge:

Relative on-ice xG (nhl_player_on_ice_rel_season) — on minus off

  • What it is: rel_xg_pct = a player's on-ice xG% minus his team's off-ice xG% (the same team with him on the bench). The honest play-driving signal: "is the team better with him on the ice than off it?"
  • Why it's more honest than raw on-ice%: raw on-ice% is confounded by team and linemate quality — a passenger on a great team posts a great on-ice%. Rel nets out the team baseline.
  • The payoff (validated, 2023-24 EDM): McDavid +9.0 and Hyman +8.9 lead by a mile; depth (Ceci −4.3, C. Brown −7.2) is clearly negative. The tell: Draisaitl's raw on-ice% looked fine (55.4%) but his Rel is −2.1 — EDM was better at 5v5 with him off that year. Raw on-ice% hid that; Rel surfaces it.
  • Ceiling: removes the team confound but still NOT competition/zone-start- adjusted (a full RAPM/WAR needs a regression); coverage limited to imported games; a mid-season trade splits a player's rows by team. The honest core of a player-value metric, not the finished WAR.
  • Tests: RelativeOnIceTests — rel == on − off, elite driver beats his team and far outdrives depth, and Rel separates McDavid from Draisaitl (unmasking).

Finishing / shooting talent (nhl_player_finishing_career) — stabilization study

  • What it is: a skater's finishing = goals scored above the xG model's expectation (GAx). The headline "is he a sniper?" number.
  • The honest question: how much of an observed GAx is repeatable skill vs luck, and how many shots until it stabilizes? Measured two independent ways (shooting_stabilization.py):
    1. Variance decomposition: per-shot Bernoulli noise s² = mean of xg(1−xg) ≈ 0.058; true-talent variance via split-half covariance ≈ 0.00011 → stabilization point n\* = s²/true_var ≈ 520 shots (reliability 0.5).
    2. Fixed-n test-retest reliability crosses 0.5 around 550–650 shots — corroborates n\*.
  • Honest signal: finishing IS a real skill, but it stabilizes slowly and the true-talent spread is small — ~1.06 GAx per 100 shots (1 SD). Most skaters are near league-average finishers; the extremes are few and need thousands of shots to confirm.
  • What this means for the leaderboard: raw GAx at the 500-shot gate is only ~49% reliable. So nhl_player_finishing_career reports regressed finishing (gax_per_100_regressed): add 520 league-average (GAx = 0) shots to shrink small samples toward the mean. The regressed leaderboard correctly keeps genuine high-volume snipers on top (Stamkos, Draisaitl, Laine, Panarin, Kucherov, Pastrnak) while pulling down small-sample flukes (e.g. a 850-shot +3.6 raw → +2.3 regressed).

Goalie rebound control (nhl_goalie_rebound_season) — honesty case study

  • What it is: rebounds a goalie leaks per save. A rebound shot (is_rebound: a shot within 3s of the prior shot) almost always follows that goalie's own save, so it measures how well they smothered the rebound.
  • THE TRAP: raw rebounds_per_100_saves has a year-over-year correlation of r = 0.873 — it looks like a rock-solid skill. It is not. A 2019-20 NHL play-by-play tracking change roughly doubled the recorded league rebound rate (~5.2 → ~11 per 100 saves) with no real change in goaltending. Consecutive seasons both drift up with the league, so the raw rate looks "stable" while mostly tracking the era, not the goalie. Ranking raw rates cross-era buried elite modern goalies (Vasilevskiy, Sorokin) as the "worst."
  • THE FIX: rank by rebounds_prevented_vs_avg — rebounds allowed versus a league-average goalie in the same season, on the same save volume (a counting stat, like GSAx). Era-neutral by construction.
  • Honest signal: once the league-season baseline is removed, the true goalie-specific year-over-year correlation is r ≈ 0.27 (r² ≈ 0.07) — rebound control is a real but modest, partly team-dependent skill, not a dominant one. The era-neutral leaderboard correctly mixes eras (Rinne, Vasilevskiy, Rask, Price on top; scrambly athletic goalies like Quick and Fleury at the bottom).
  • Lesson: a high year-over-year correlation is NOT evidence of skill when a shared time trend (here, a tracking change) inflates it. Always compare to the same-period league baseline before claiming persistence.
  • Tests: GoalieReboundControlTests — including test_tracking_era_break_exists (locks in the confound so it can't silently vanish) and test_rebound_control_is_modestly_stable (asserts 0.10 < r < 0.60, guarding against both "it's noise" and "it's era leakage").

Score / game-state adjustment (nhl_team_score_adjusted_season)

  • What it is: teams play to the score, so raw shot/xG totals are biased by game script. This removes that bias. Each shot gets its score state (the shooter's lead/deficit, capped ±3) derived from goal_events (running score) joined to game_events (sort order); a monotonic window gives the score just before each shot.
  • The score effect (validated): trailing teams take more shots (~43k vs ~37k for leading in a season) at lower quality (xG/shot 0.070 trailing-2+ → 0.085 leading-2+; sh% 6.1% → 8.7%). Leading teams sit back and get fewer but higher-quality (rush) chances.
  • The correction: weight each shot by w(state) = 0.5 / (league FOR-share of shots in that state). Weights are symmetric (tied = 1.0), monotone (~0.90 trailing-3 → ~1.13 leading-3), and the same weight applies to a shot's xGF (shooter) and xGA (defender, who is in the mirror state). Pooled-league weights are baked into the view; a test recomputes them and fails on >5% drift.
  • Validation: score-adjusted xG-share leaders are the known analytics-elite possession teams (Carolina, Florida, Edmonton, Colorado, Dallas). Adjustment nudges good teams up (they led more, so game script deflated their raw share).
  • Ceiling: team-season, all strengths pooled; not split by 5v5 vs special teams or venue. Materialized to nhl_team_score_adjusted_season_cache (the per-shot window is ~8s) via warehouse_maintenance.py refresh-nhl-score-adjusted.
  • Tests: test_nhl_score_adjustment.py — score effects exist, weights symmetric + near-baked (drift guard), 1:1 row count, plausible leaders, routing.

  • Calibration: win-probability divisor fit to history (580, wider than the textbook 400) so predicted win rates match observed; well-calibrated by decile.
  • Validation: peak-Elo teams and biggest upsets match known history.

How to add a metric here

  1. State what it measures and the honest ceiling.
  2. Report a calibration or reliability number (Brier/log loss, decile error, split-half / year-over-year correlation, or a validated leaderboard).
  3. Check for confounds before claiming persistence — especially era/tracking trends, volume, and team context. Compare to a same-period baseline.
  4. Add a test that locks the finding in (and, where relevant, locks in the confound itself so a future data change is caught).
