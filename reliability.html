Contract projections — graded against reality

For every real signing we re-project the deal using only comps signed in earlier years (no hindsight), then compare to what the player actually got. This is the comparable-signings projector behind the cap simulator, held to account.

Trade-ask report card — expected price vs actual, walk-forward

Every clean-headliner trade since 2013 is scored on what our market lens would have quoted the day it happened (comps strictly from earlier trades) vs the package he actually fetched — both in pick-WAR7. Three findings shape the product: the naive tier×control lookup BEAT the similarity engine; recency weighting was refused — every half-life tested made prices worse, because the units (cap-%, era-relative percentiles) already absorb the era; and forward price QUOTES were retired from player cards the same day they shipped — the ledger is censored exactly where readers look (2 clean sales of under-24 controlled players in sixteen years), so this audit scores deals retrospectively and quotes nothing.

How to read these numbers

WAR (wins above replacement) claims each player adds a measurable number of wins. The test of that claim is external: sum every player's WAR per team and compare the totals to the team's actual goal differential. The correlation between those two lists is the r you see quoted — 0 means no relationship, 1 means perfect lockstep. At r ≈ 0.85 (r² ≈ 0.72), about 72% of the gap between good and bad teams is explained purely by adding up our individual player ratings. The remaining ~28% is honest residue: goalie streaks, shooting luck, injuries, and noise.

Why it matters: the model never sees the standings — it only sees shots and shifts. When its player sums independently reproduce the standings, the units are real wins, not vibes. That is what licenses pricing a win on the cap market — currently $4.71M per WAR for skaters (re-fit 2026-06-27) and $1.99M per WAR for goalies (re-fit 2026-07-01, RS-only after the game-type re-pin), fit through the league-minimum floor and re-fit as the model evolves (it was $5.25M before the finishing fold-in) — and grading GMs against it. One honest footnote: the fit is 0.88–0.91 in recent seasons but 0.65–0.82 in the early 2010s — same model, noisier old shot data, weaker fit. That pattern is itself evidence the validation measures something real rather than being tuned to flatter.

Where WAR is blind

5v5 xG-RAPM compresses elite defensemen and high-volume finishers — the isolated on-ice estimate under-credits the very top of both groups. That is why the value tier runs on total WAR and player cards flag the gap where it bites.

Isolated / defensive RAPM travels worse across a team change than box production (0.78 vs 0.90 retention) — which is why no RAPM-based fit engine is shipped. And single-season RAPM is noise-dominated: the 3-season pool is the stable read; treat any one season alone as a hint, not a verdict.
